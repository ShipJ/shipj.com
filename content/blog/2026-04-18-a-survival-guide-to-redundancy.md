If redundancy has just landed in your lap, this is a reminder that it can be the start of a better chapter - not the end of the story.

Redundancy was meant to feel like failure, rejection, panic, not being good enough wrapped up in one HR meeting. But when it happened to me, the first emotion was <u>relief</u>.

The 12 months leading up to that point were intense

I’d been pushing through a period where the work no longer matched the effort I was putting in, and the effort no longer matched what I wanted my life to look like. The routine was familiar, the paycheque was dependable, and the identity was comfortable—but the trajectory felt off. Redundancy didn’t create a problem - it created clarity.

## What redundancy feels like

Redundancy is rarely one clean emotion. It’s more like a messy playlist on shuffle:

- **Relief **- a decision was made for me that I probably wouldn’t 

- **Shock**, because your nervous system didn’t see it coming—even if your brain did.

- **Anger **- because it’s impersonal and yet deeply personal.

- **Shame**, because our culture treats job loss like a moral failing.

- **Fear**, because uncertainty is expensive.

- **Excitement**, because possibilities reappear.

If you’re reading this shortly after being told your role is going, the most important thing to know is: *whatever you’re feeling is normal*. Your reaction isn’t a verdict on your ability; it’s your mind trying to quickly make sense of a sudden change.

## What to do about it



The first mistake people (myself included) are tempted to make is to jump straight into action mode: update the CV, message everyone, apply everywhere, build a plan.

A plan helps, but only after you have stability.

For the first 24–48 hours, try to reduce the noise and protect your headspace.

- **Sleep and eat** something resembling real food.

- **Tell one or two people** you trust so you’re not carrying it alone.

- **Avoid doom-scrolling** job boards as a form of self-punishment.

- **Write down the facts** while they’re fresh: dates, package details, any written comms you’ve received, what you were told verbally.

You can’t make good decisions while your body thinks it’s in danger. Give yourself permission to treat this like a real event.



You don’t need to become a finance wiz overnight, but you do need a clear view of your runway:

- What is your **notice period** and when does it end?

- What is the **redundancy package** (and when does it land)?

- What are your **monthly essentials** (rent/mortgage, bills, food, childcare)?

- What costs can you **pause or reduce** without making life miserable?

- What’s the minimum you need each month to stay stable?

This isn’t about fear—it’s about agency. Clarity turns “I’m panicking” into “I have X months and here are my options.”

## Step 3: Separate “job loss” from “self-worth”

This is the hardest bit, because redundancy pokes at identity.

You can do excellent work and still be made redundant. You can be valuable and still be unaffordable. You can be the wrong shape for the business model and still be the right shape for the world.

Redundancy is often driven by:

- a re-org

- a budget cut

- a shift in priorities

- a merger

- a change in leadership

- a bet that didn’t pay off

None of those are a measurement of your potential. They are a measurement of a company’s current constraints and decisions.

If you only take one mindset shift from this article, let it be this:

**Redundancy is an organisational outcome, not a personal diagnosis.**

## Step 4: Decide what you actually want next

There are two different “next steps” people take:

1. **Replace the job quickly** (speed and stability)

1. **Use the gap to reposition** (fit and trajectory)

Neither is morally superior. The right move depends on your runway, responsibilities, and mental bandwidth.

A few questions that helped me:

- What parts of my last role did I *genuinely* enjoy?

- What parts drained me, even on “good” weeks?

- If nothing changed for another two years, would I be okay with that?

- What do I want my day-to-day to look like—not just my title?

- What kind of team environment brings out my best work?

- What am I unwilling to tolerate again?

Write the answers down. Redundancy can be a rare moment where you’re forced to choose instead of simply continuing.

## Step 5: Build your “redundancy story” (clear, calm, confident)

You will be asked about it—by recruiters, by hiring managers, by friends and family. Having a simple, rehearsed explanation protects your energy.

Here are a few versions you can adapt:

- “My role was made redundant as part of a wider restructure. I’m now looking for a role focused on X.”

- “The company reduced headcount and my team was affected. I’m using this moment to move towards Y.”

- “A shift in priorities meant my role wasn’t needed long-term. I’m excited to bring my experience in A and B to a new team.”

Keep it factual, non-defensive, and forward-looking. You don’t owe anyone the full emotional truth.

## Step 6: Take action that compounds (not action that numbs)

When you’re anxious, it’s easy to do “busy work” that looks productive but doesn’t move anything forward.

Compounding actions are small steps that build momentum:

- Update your LinkedIn headline and *about* section to match what you want next.

- Reconnect with 5 people you genuinely like working with (not 50 strangers).

- Pick 2–3 target role types and tailor for them, instead of applying randomly.

- Create a simple portfolio of proof (a one-page case study beats a perfect CV).

- Apply consistently in a small daily block rather than marathon sessions.

- Get feedback early—before you’ve sent 30 applications into the void.

The goal is not to be frantic. The goal is to be steady.

## What helped me emotionally (and might help you too)

I didn’t “stay positive” the whole time. That’s not the point. The point is to stay grounded.

A few things that made a difference:

- **Routine**, even a light one. A walk. A morning coffee. A set time to job search.

- **Boundaries**, especially around who I told and how much I explained.

- **Movement**, because anxiety loves stillness.

- **Talking to someone**, whether a friend, coach, therapist, or mentor.

- **Writing**, because it turns spirals into sentences—and sentences into plans.

Redundancy can make you feel powerless. Small, repeatable habits give you power back.

## When redundancy becomes the best outcome

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: sometimes redundancy is the cleanest exit you were never going to choose.

It can be the thing that:

- breaks a pattern you’ve outgrown

- forces a needed reset

- gives you time to think

- pushes you to bet on yourself

- makes you confront what you actually want

Not always. Not immediately. But often.

If you’re in the middle of it, you don’t need to spin it into a motivational poster. You just need to keep moving—one grounded decision at a time.

And if today feels heavy, that’s okay.

You’re not behind. You’re not broken. You’re just between chapters.
