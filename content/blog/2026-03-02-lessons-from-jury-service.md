I was a fresh-faced grad when the first letter arrived - *Jury Service Summons*. Deferred. I still had the sense that adulthood came with a certificate you got handed at reception, alongside a lanyard and a map. I had barely learned how to sound confident in meetings. I was still surprised when people asked for my opinion on anything that mattered.

I always wanted to 

### “Are you sure you’ve got the right person?”

I kept rereading the instructions, half-expecting a line that said *if you are underprepared, underqualified, or prone to overthinking, please return this form immediately.* There was no such line.

The language was blunt and practical. Turn up. Listen. Don’t discuss the case. Decide.

That last word sat heavy. *Decide.*

In university, decisions had redo buttons. You could resubmit coursework. You could retake an exam. Even the bigger choices felt reversible in a few years, with a different job, a different city, a different version of yourself.

This was different. This was consequential in a way my CV had never prepared me for.

### The imposter syndrome of responsibility

Everyone talks about imposter syndrome at work, but I did not realise how far it could travel. It followed me straight into the courtroom, dressed up as civic duty.

I remember looking around at the other jurors and thinking they all looked more qualified than me. Not necessarily older. Just… steadier. Like they already knew the rules of the world.

I felt like a kid who had wandered into the wrong lecture theatre and now had to sit an exam anyway.

The strange thing is, no one on a jury is “qualified” in the way you mean when you are fresh out of university. You are not there because you have special knowledge. You are there because you don’t.

The whole point is that you are ordinary.

And when you are young, being ordinary can feel like being unprepared.

### Listening properly is harder than it looks

Jury service is mostly waiting, which I did not expect. Waiting in corridors. Waiting to be called in. Waiting while procedures click into place. It gives you far too much time to imagine what you will do wrong.

Then, suddenly, everything speeds up. Evidence. Questions. Details you will be expected to remember later.

I realised quickly that “just listen” is not a simple instruction. Listening properly means holding multiple truths at once.

It means paying attention even when you are tired.

It means noticing what is said *and* what is not said.

It means resisting the temptation to invent a story that makes everything feel neat.

And it means accepting that you will not get certainty. You get what was presented, what was challenged, what was clarified, and what remains ambiguous.

### The weight of the decision

The most unsettling part was how normal the mechanics of it all felt.

You sit in a room. You talk. You debate. You ask each other questions. You try to be fair. You try to be careful.

From the outside, it could look like any group decision I had ever been part of.

But the stakes transformed every sentence. There was a person behind it all, waiting.

That is where the fear came from, I think. Not from the law or the formality, but from the human reality: your conclusion becomes part of someone’s life.

When you have never been trusted with something that heavy before, it is hard to believe you should be trusted now.

### What I learned (and what surprised me)

I went in thinking jury service would prove I was out of my depth.

Instead, it taught me something quietly reassuring:

- Responsibility is not reserved for people who feel ready.

- Serious decisions are rarely made with perfect information.

- Being careful, slow, and willing to question your own assumptions is a kind of competence.

I also learned that humility can be useful. Not the self-deprecating kind that shuts you down, but the kind that keeps you open. The kind that says, *I might be wrong, so I will pay attention.*

### Leaving with a different definition of “qualified”

By the end, I still did not feel like an expert. But I stopped believing that expertise was what was being asked of me.

The system was not looking for a flawless decision-maker. It was looking for a group of people willing to do a difficult thing conscientiously.

And that, inconveniently, is something you can do even as a fresh-faced grad.

Not because you are fearless.

Because you show up anyway.
