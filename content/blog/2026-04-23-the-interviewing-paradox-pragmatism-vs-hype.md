There’s nothing like the awkward tension of modern interviews - one that is rarely acknowledged but felt by everyone who walks into the room (or joins the call). On one hand, you’re expected to come across as grounded, rational, pragmatic, measured - someone who makes smart decisions, gauges risk, and appreciates trade-offs. On the other, you’re expected to fly the flag for the latest tech, enthusiastic about emerging trends, and ready to champion whatever comes next. To me, these expectations collide but why does that matter?

A hiring manager may probe into how you approach system design, and ‘safe’ answers might lean towards reliability in proven design patterns. But in the next breath, they probe your experience with the newest frameworks, AI tooling, or architectural paradigms, looking for experience beyond the length of its existence (FastAPI anyone?).

Suddenly, stability is outdated. Emphasising caution risks sounding stagnant. If you emphasise novelty, you’re ungrounded.

You find yourself carefully calibrating every answer. When discussing past projects, you highlight pragmatic decisions—why you didn’t over-engineer, why you chose boring but reliable tools. But you quickly pivot to mention how you’ve been experimenting with newer technologies on the side, staying “current,” keeping pace. You’re trying to signal two things at once: that you won’t recklessly chase trends, and that you won’t fall behind them either.

This expectation reflects a broader uncertainty in the industry - organisations want innovation, but not instability. They want people who can adopt new tools quickly, but won’t compromise existing systems. Curiosity without chaos.

For candidates, this creates a subtle identity problem. Are you being evaluated as a steady operator or as an early adopter? As someone who questions hype, or someone who drives it? Often, the answer is “both,” but the interview format doesn’t reward nuance. There’s a performative aspect to enthusiasm. Being *excited* about new technology is mandatory, even when your perspective is more subdued and context-dependent. You might believe a new tool has promise but isn’t production-ready, yet the interview nudges you toward even more advocacy. Underplaying excitement is seen as disengagement, while overplaying it feels inauthentic.

So what? They learn to frame pragmatism as a strength—“I evaluate new tools carefully and adopt them where they add clear value”—while still signaling forward momentum—“I’ve been exploring X, Y, and Z and see real potential in specific use cases.” It’s less about choosing a side and more about demonstrating controlled flexibility.

Perhaps the deeper issue is that interviews compress complex professional judgment into short conversations. In reality, being both pragmatic and forward-looking isn’t contradictory—it’s the job. Good engineers, designers, and leaders constantly negotiate that boundary. They experiment, but they also restrain. They adopt, but they also question.

The difficulty is not in holding both perspectives. It’s in proving that you can, in the limited bandwidth of an interview.

Maybe the more honest approach—both for candidates and interviewers—is to acknowledge the tension rather than smooth it over. To talk explicitly about trade-offs. To admit where new technologies excite you and where they make you uneasy. To show that being “up to speed” doesn’t mean uncritical enthusiasm, and that being “pragmatic” doesn’t mean resistant to change.

The best people I’ve worked with don’t pick a side; they know when to play for each.
